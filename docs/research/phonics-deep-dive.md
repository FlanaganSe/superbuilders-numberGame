## 1. Elkonin Sound Boxes / Say-It-Move-It

### Origin and mechanism

D.B. Elkonin (1963), a Soviet developmental psychologist, invented the method. A child hears a word, then moves one physical token into a drawn square for each phoneme. The physical displacement externalizes what is otherwise a purely mental phonological operation — each phoneme becomes countable, spatially distinct, and sequentially ordered. The boxes scaffold working memory: the child does not have to hold all phonemes in mind simultaneously; they offload them into the physical world one by one.

Say-It-Move-It is the most widely used instructional implementation: (1) teacher says the word, (2) child repeats it while placing a token per phoneme (segmenting), (3) child runs a finger across the placed tokens while saying each sound, (4) child sweeps all tokens back while saying the whole word (blending).

### Research evidence

**Ross & Joseph (2018) — systematic review:** Found "evidence to support the use of word boxes for helping preschool to elementary students acquire phonemic awareness, letter-sound correspondences, and spelling." This is the primary synthesis cited by NASET's LD Report on Elkonin Boxes as an evidence-based practice.
Source: [naset.com — Evidence Based Practice Research: Elkonin Boxes](https://www.naset.com/publications/ld-report/evidence-based-practice-research-elkonin-boxes/)

**Reading Rockets:** Classifies Elkonin Boxes as an evidence-based classroom strategy. Documents that it benefits typically developing students, English Language Learners, and students at risk for reading difficulties.
Source: [readingrockets.org — Elkonin Boxes](https://www.readingrockets.org/classroom/classroom-strategies/elkonin-boxes)

**Colorado DPI / CEEDAR:** Publish Say-It-and-Move-It as a formal instructional routine for K–2, noting that "blending and segmentation are the phonemic awareness skills that have the most impact on later reading ability, and for this reason, the majority of instruction provided in early elementary involves these two skills."
Source: [cde.state.co.us — Say It and Move It](https://www.cde.state.co.us/sites/default/files/documents/coloradoliteracy/clf/downloads/say_it_and_move_it.pdf)

**Evidence level:** Multiple systematic reviews; endorsed by WWC practice guides for K–3.

**Age range:** Pre-K through early elementary. The blank-box/counter stage is appropriate for children who do not yet know letter-sound correspondences (ages 4–5). The letter-box stage (child writes or places one grapheme per box) requires basic grapheme-phoneme knowledge — appropriate from approximately mid-kindergarten (age 5.5–6).

### Three-stage progression

Lane (University of Florida / UFLI) documents a formal three-stage Elkonin progression:
1. **Phoneme stage:** blank boxes + counters or tokens (no letters) — segmentation only
2. **Letter stage:** child writes one grapheme per box — segmentation plus grapheme encoding
3. **Word study stage:** boxes map to orthographic units (rimes, morphemes) — for readers transitioning to consolidated alphabetic phase

The game's tile-placement mechanic is structurally the letter-box stage. A child placing physical letter tiles left-to-right into a spatial sequence is performing a validated phonological task, not a novel or arbitrary one.
Source: [ufli.education.ufl.edu — Using Elkonin Boxes (Lane)](https://ufli.education.ufl.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/Using-Elkonin-Boxes-Handout.pdf)

### Mapping to physical letter tiles

The critical difference between a standard Elkonin letter-box exercise and the game's mechanic: the game requires the child to *select* the correct letter tile (encoding from memory), while a standard letter-box exercise may show the word first and have the child write what they see (copy). The game's mechanic, when the word is withheld from the child and presented only as a spoken or pictured prompt, matches the highest-efficacy variant described by Ehri — the retrieval-from-phonological-analysis step that creates orthographic mapping (see Section 2).

## 2. Encoding (Spelling) as a Reading Instruction Strategy

### Causal relationship: the evidence

**Weiser & Mathes (2011) — best-evidence synthesis:**
Title: "Using Encoding Instruction to Improve the Reading and Spelling Performances of Elementary Students At Risk for Literacy Difficulties: A Best-Evidence Synthesis"
Journal: *Review of Educational Research*, Vol. 81, No. 2, pp. 170–200.
Synthesized 11 stringently filtered studies.

Key findings:
- Encoding instruction produced significant positive effects across: phonemic awareness, spelling, decoding, fluency, reading comprehension, and writing
- Encoding and decoding are "reciprocally (or even synergistically)" related — teaching spelling improves reading; teaching reading improves spelling
- Transfer effects documented: early encoding instruction predicts later reading performance at end-of-year assessments
- Applicable to at-risk primary-grade students and older students with learning disabilities

Source: [journals.sagepub.com — Weiser & Mathes 2011](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0034654310396719) / [eric.ed.gov EJ923791](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ923791)

**LSHSS (2023) — prospective study:**
"Recent evidence suggests a unique role for spelling in prompting early reading development *beyond* other foundational literacy variables such as phoneme awareness and letter-sound knowledge." Specifically: "nonword spelling was a unique predictor of later (end of year) reading and played a facilitative role in the emergence of decoding."
Source: [pubs.asha.org — The Relationship Between Early Spelling and Decoding](https://pubs.asha.org/doi/10.1044/2023_LSHSS-22-00161)

**Evidence level:** Strong. WWC rates combined decoding + encoding instruction at Strong Evidence for K–3 (Recommendation 3 of the Foundational Skills to Support Reading for Understanding in Kindergarten Through 3rd Grade practice guide).

### Ehri (2014) — Orthographic Mapping: exact prerequisites

**Full citation:** Ehri, L.C. (2014). Orthographic mapping in the acquisition of sight word reading, spelling memory, and vocabulary learning. *Scientific Studies of Reading*, 18(1), 5–21.
DOI: 10.1080/10888438.2013.819356
Source: [tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10888438.2013.819356) / [eric.ed.gov EJ1027413](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1027413)

From the abstract and ERIC record: orthographic mapping "is enabled by phonemic awareness and grapheme-phoneme knowledge."

The two necessary prerequisites:

1. **Phonemic awareness** — specifically the ability to segment spoken words into individual phonemes and blend phonemes back into whole words. Ehri specifies that segmentation and blending are the relevant sub-skills; rhyme awareness alone is not sufficient.

2. **Grapheme-phoneme knowledge** — mastery of the letter-sound correspondences of the writing system. The child must know which letters map to which sounds.

Ehri's instructional guidelines (derived from the 2014 paper, summarized by understandingreading.home.blog) add a third procedural requirement:

3. **Active retrieval during encoding** — the child must *attempt to retrieve* the spelling by applying phoneme-grapheme knowledge, not passively copy a visible model. The retrieval attempt is the learning event. Guideline 4 specifies: "(a) pronounce the word and count phonemes, (b) view the spelling and match graphemes to sounds, (c) recall the analysis and write the word from memory." Step (c) — writing from memory, not from copy — is what creates the orthographic bond.

Source: [understandingreading.home.blog — Ehri's instructional guidelines](https://understandingreading.home.blog/2021/04/18/dr-linnea-ehris-list-of-instructional-guidelines-for-enhancing-orthographic-mapping-and-word-learning/)

**Mechanism:** Orthographic mapping is how words become permanent sight words — not through rote repetition but through successful phonological analysis of the written form. When a child hears "cat," segments /k/ /æ/ /t/, and then identifies that C maps to /k/, A maps to /æ/, T maps to /t/, the three-way bond (spelling–pronunciation–meaning) is formed in long-term memory. Skilled readers have 30,000–60,000 such mappings.

**Critical implication for the game:** The game must require the child to retrieve and select letters without seeing the target word simultaneously. A child who is shown "CAT" on screen while placing tiles is copying, not mapping. The retrieval attempt itself is the OM event.

### Invented spelling evidence

- Experimental studies show invented-spelling training produces "greater progress in phonemic tests than control groups" and that the invented-spelling group "learned to read more words in a learn-to-read task than other groups" (Ouellette & Sénéchal, ScienceDirect).
  Source: [sciencedirect.com — Impact of Invented Spelling on Phonemic Awareness](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475205000939)

- "Invented-spelling training benefited phonological and orthographic awareness" — the effect extends beyond phonemic awareness to spelling memory itself.
  Source: [jowr.org — Invented spelling as a tool to develop early literacy](https://www.jowr.org/jowr/article/download/857/876/1047)

- Kindergarteners who use phonetic spelling in first and second grades perform better in reading and spelling than those who do not.
  Source: [scholarworks.uni.edu](https://scholarworks.uni.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1603&context=grp)

- Invented-spelling practice can enhance phonemic awareness "to a degree comparable to that achieved through explicit phonemic awareness curriculum."
  Source: [academia.edu — Ouellette et al.](https://www.academia.edu/100972151/The_impact_of_invented_spelling_on_phonemic_awareness)

**Evidence level:** Moderate-to-strong. Multiple experimental studies show a causal, bidirectional effect: phonemic awareness supports spelling, and spelling practice develops phonemic awareness.

## 3. CVC Word Progression for Phonics Instruction

### Structural definition

CVC = Consonant-Vowel-Consonant, with the vowel producing its short sound. Examples: cat, pig, hop, bed, sun, cup. These are the first decodable words taught in every evidence-based phonics sequence because:
- All three phonemes are phonemically transparent (each letter maps to one sound)
- The vowel follows a predictable short-sound pattern
- Three phonemes is within the segmentation capacity of children age 5–6

### Research-backed instructional sequence

The following progression is consistent across IDA, UFLI, NAEP, and state literacy frameworks:

1. Single-letter consonants + short vowels → CVC words
2. CVCC (e.g., "band") and CCVC (e.g., "stop") — simple final/initial consonant clusters
3. Digraphs — sh, ch, th, wh, ck (two letters, one sound)
4. Silent-E (CVCe) — long vowels via silent-e pattern
5. Long vowel teams — ai, ay, ee, ea, oa, etc.
6. Diphthongs and advanced vowel patterns

Source: [braintrusttutors.com — Phonics Instructional Sequence](https://braintrusttutors.com/the-instructional-sequence-of-phonics-rules/) / [improvingliteracy.org — How to Use Systematic Phonics Instruction](https://improvingliteracy.org/resource/how-to-use-systematic-phonics-instruction-in-your-classroom/)

The guiding principle: "relationships that enable students to begin reading words as quickly as possible should be introduced earliest." No single universally agreed-upon sequence exists across curricula, but CVC-first is universal and undisputed.

### Short vowel order

The Fairleigh Dickinson Center for Dyslexia Studies recommends introducing short vowels in this order: **a → i → o → e → u**.

Rationale: avoid adjacent instruction of acoustically and visually similar vowels. Short /i/ and short /e/ are the most easily confused pair (both are front mid vowels; spelled with visually similar letters); placing them non-adjacent (positions 2 and 4) reduces interference.

Short /a/ is additionally the most common short vowel in high-frequency early CVC words (cat, map, hat, can, bad, sat, van) and is acoustically distinct — a logical starting point regardless of curriculum.

Source: [fromsoundstospelling.com — In What Order Do You Teach Vowels](https://www.fromsoundstospelling.com/blog/in-what-order-do-you-teach-vowels)

### Word families (rimes) vs. mixed CVC: the research verdict

**Johnston & Watson (2004)** — controlled experiment comparing three approaches with 5-year-olds, 16 weeks, 20 min/day:
- Group A: Synthetic phonics (grapheme-phoneme focus, no phoneme awareness component)
- Group B: Analytic phonics (word family / rime focus) + phoneme awareness training
- Group C: Analytic phonics (word family / rime focus) alone

Results:
- Synthetic phonics group: spelling **7 months ahead** of chronological age at end of kindergarten
- Analytic phonics groups: spelling **2–3 months behind** chronological age
- Only the synthetic phonics group could read by analogy and performed better on irregular words and nonwords
- 7-year follow-up (Clackmannanshire study): effect sizes for word identification increased year over year

Source: [thereadingleague.org — Brady 2020 Alphabetics Synthesis](https://www.thereadingleague.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/Brady-Expanded-Version-of-Alphabetics-TRLJ.pdf)

**Why word families underperform:** Children taught exclusively within a single word family (e.g., -at: cat, bat, mat, hat) learn to recognize the rime pattern and guess from the onset — they may not process the medial vowel. When these same words appear in a mixed list or in text, decoding fails because the child skips internal phoneme analysis. Research confirms: "children may successfully read a list of word family words but then can't decode one of the same words when it's in a text or in a different list of mixed words."
Source: [fromsoundstospelling.com — 3 Mistakes to Avoid When Teaching Word Families](https://www.fromsoundstospelling.com/blog/3-mistakes-to-avoid-when-teaching-word-families)

**Recommendation for the game:** Group words by *vowel sound* (all short-/a/ words initially), not by rime family. As the child gains facility with a vowel, mix words from different word families within the same vowel class (cat, map, van — not cat, bat, mat). Once two vowels are established, mix across vowel classes to require full grapheme-phoneme analysis on every word.

## 4. Audio + Physical Manipulation for Literacy

### Multisensory phonics instruction: the research base

Multisensory structured literacy (Orton-Gillingham and its derivatives) combines auditory (hearing phonemes), visual (seeing letters), and kinesthetic-tactile (physically tracing, writing, or handling letter tiles) channels simultaneously. The International Dyslexia Association endorses this approach; it is the standard of care for students with dyslexia and is also effective for typical learners.

**Reading Rockets synthesis:** "Teaching experience supports a multi-sensory instruction approach in the early grades to improve phonemic awareness, phonics, and reading comprehension skills. Including manipulatives, gestures, and speaking and auditory cues increases students' acquisition of phonics skills."
Source: [readingrockets.org — Phonics Instruction: Value of Multi-sensory Approach](https://www.readingrockets.org/topics/curriculum-and-instruction/articles/phonics-instruction-value-multi-sensory-approach)

**IMSE (Institute for Multi-Sensory Education):** "Positive effects of multisensory learning activities are evident in student learning... Multi-sensory activities provide needed scaffolding to beginning and struggling readers."
Source: [journal.imse.com — Multi-Sensory Learning Types](https://journal.imse.com/multi-sensory-learning-types-of-instruction-and-materials/)

### Specific evidence: letter + sound simultaneous presentation

**French study (n=36, ages 5–6):** Multisensory letter integration (visual + tactile + auditory simultaneously) significantly improved letter shape acquisition and decoding skill compared to a visual-only condition. Cited in multisensory digital phonics research published in MDPI Education Sciences.
Source: [mdpi.com — Multisensory Interactive Digital Text for Bilingual Beginning Readers](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/12/11/750)

**Temporal contiguity principle (Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning):** For multimodal learning to produce deeper memory encoding, the auditory and visual channels must be activated simultaneously, not sequentially. The gap between seeing a letter tile and hearing its phoneme must be less than approximately 1 second for the cross-modal association to form.

### Tangible User Interfaces (TUIs) for literacy

**Systematic review (Springer, Personal and Ubiquitous Computing, 2021):** Identified 155 studies (2001–2019) on TUIs in early-years education. Key findings:
- Manipulatives are the dominant form of tangible object studied
- TUI conditions outperform "classical learning methods" — students "explore alternative proposals and become more involved" and "problems and the path to a solution are perceived as fun"
- Physical object affordances activate engagement that touchscreen-only interfaces do not

Source: [link.springer.com — TUI in early years systematic review](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00779-021-01556-x)

**PhonoBlocks (TUI for ELL literacy):** 3D tangible letter tiles that change color as sounds change. Chinese children learning English as a foreign language achieved "significant learning gains relative to their baseline performance" after PhonoBlocks instruction in letter-sound correspondences. Cited in the Springer TUI systematic review.

**TUI for literacy in Down syndrome:** Multiple experimental studies confirm that tangible interfaces improve literacy outcomes for children with Down syndrome. The tactile channel is particularly important when the auditory and visual channels are processing-limited.
Source: [link.springer.com — TUIs for Down Syndrome literacy](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-05039-8_23)

**TUI for dyslexia:** Systematic literature review of dyslexia-friendly TUI design features confirms that physical manipulation supports phoneme-grapheme learning specifically, not only reading fluency.
Source: [rsisinternational.org — Dyslexic Learners and Tangible Educational Products](https://rsisinternational.org/journals/ijriss/articles/empowering-dyslexic-learners-a-design-approach-to-tangible-educational-products/)

### Is tactile + auditory > either alone?

The evidence directionally supports multimodal superiority, especially for struggling readers. The physical tile is not merely motivational — it serves as an external working memory scaffold. While the auditory channel processes phoneme identity and the visual channel processes letter identity, the proprioceptive channel (placing the tile) encodes position and sequence. The three channels create convergent memory traces that are more retrievable than a single-channel encoding.

The effect is stronger for children with reading difficulties than for typical developers, suggesting a ceiling effect — typically developing children can succeed with fewer channels. For the target age range (5–8), the physical manipulation is most valuable during the initial learning of grapheme-phoneme correspondences, before those correspondences become automatic.

**Evidence level:** Moderate. Experimental evidence exists for specific populations and implementations. The mechanism (multisensory memory encoding) is well established in cognitive science; the literacy-specific applications are replicated but with smaller sample sizes than would qualify for Strong Evidence under WWC criteria.

## 5. Image + Word Association for Vocabulary in Early Readers

### Evidence for showing pictures alongside words (ages 5–6)

**Dual coding theory (Paivio 1971; Clark & Paivio 1991):** Verbal labels and visual images are processed by two separate but interconnected memory systems (verbal and imagery). Encoding information in both systems creates two independent retrieval paths. Either code can activate the other. When a child sees the word "cat" alongside a picture of a cat, the visual concept and the verbal label are stored with mutual connectivity, making both more retrievable.

"Supplying two modes of representation — both visual and verbal — facilitates learning across major components of language acquisition from vocabulary and grammar knowledge to application in oral and written modalities."
Source: [Clark & Paivio (1991) — Dual Coding Theory and Education](https://nschwartz.yourweb.csuchico.edu/Clark%20&%20Paivio.pdf)

**For young children (~age 8) specifically:** "Learning foreign language vocabulary with gestures and pictures enhances vocabulary memory for several months post-learning in eight-year-old school children." The effect persists beyond the learning session — it is not transient.
Source: [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov — Dual Coding or Cognitive Load, EFL Vocabulary](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8963493/)

**Spatial contiguity principle (meta-analysis of 37 studies):** Text and image must be presented together — "separating text and images consistently leads to worse retention and transfer performance" — large mean weighted effect size (d = 0.72). Images placed far from their corresponding text labels lose most of their dual-coding benefit.
Source: [sciencedirect.com — Picture-word integration and reading](https://www.sciencedirect.com/article/abs/pii/S0959475220306927)

**Evidence level:** Strong for vocabulary acquisition. Dual coding is one of the most replicated findings in educational psychology. The mechanism is well established across age ranges.

### The picture dependency risk: what the research actually shows

**The risk is documented and quantified.** A review of 37 experiments on picture-text compounds in reading instruction found:
- 64.9% of experiments reported *detrimental* effects of picture-text compounds on word-reading performance
- Pictures can function as a word-bypass strategy: children who cannot decode accurately use the image to guess the word, producing correct answers in pictured contexts that fail in text-only contexts

Source: [link.springer.com — Picture-Text Compounds in Early Reading: Descriptive Review](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42822-023-00139-0)

**Mechanism of harm:** When a child sees a picture of a dog beside the word "dog," the most cognitively economical strategy is to identify the picture and bypass the graphophonemic analysis entirely. This strategy works in pictured contexts and appears correct — but the child has not processed the written word, so no orthographic mapping occurs. As texts become harder and illustrations thin out, this strategy fails.

**Condition where pictures help rather than harm:** Picture-embedded words (where the image is incorporated into the letterforms of the word itself) combined with explicit phonics instruction significantly improved retention over text-alone conditions. The key difference: the picture cue was linked to a specific phoneme, not used as a whole-word substitute.
Source: [sciencedirect.com — Picture-embedded method for sight word acquisition](https://www.sciencedirect.com/article/pii/S0959475218308430)

**The at-risk caveat (PediaSpeech):** Picture dependency is particularly a risk for children who lack adequate phonological awareness skills. For typically developing readers, pictures provide a helpful semantic frame. For struggling readers without phonological awareness, pictures become a substitute for decoding — and the substitution is difficult to unlearn.
Source: [pediaspeech.com — Using Pictures with Beginning Readers](https://www.pediaspeech.com/using-pictures-illustrations-with-beginning-readers-is-it-helpful-or-hurtful/)

### Implication for game design

Pictures should serve one purpose: communicating what the word *means* (semantic/vocabulary function), not what the word *says* (phonological decoding function). The image should accompany the spoken word as a semantic anchor, presented *before* the child attempts to spell — not as a cue the child can use to guess which letters to place. The phonological analysis must be required; the picture is not permitted to substitute for it.

Concretely: show a picture of a cat + say "cat" aloud at the start of the round. Then remove or blur the picture while the child selects tiles. The picture scaffolds vocabulary comprehension; the phonological work is still required for spelling.

## 6. Developmental Sequence for Phonemic Awareness

### Hierarchy of skills: easiest to hardest

This progression is replicated across all major developmental research literature (Chaney 1992; Snider 1997; Warrick et al.; Torgesen & Mathes):

| Skill | Description | Typical emergence |
|---|---|---|
| Word awareness | Identifying separate words in a spoken sentence | Age 3–4 |
| Rhyme recognition | Recognizing that "cat" and "hat" rhyme | Age 4–4.5 |
| Rhyme production | Generating a word that rhymes with a given word | Age 4.5–5 |
| Syllable segmentation | Clapping or counting syllables in a word | Age 4–5 |
| Onset-rime awareness | Recognizing that "cat" = /k/ + /at/ | Age 5 |
| Phoneme isolation | Identifying the first or last sound in a word | Age 5–5.5 |
| Phoneme blending | Combining /k/ /æ/ /t/ into "cat" | Age 5–6 |
| Phoneme segmentation | Breaking "cat" into /k/ /æ/ /t/ | Age 5.5–6.5 |
| Phoneme manipulation | Deletion, substitution, transposition | Age 6–7 |

Sources: [aussiechildcarenetwork.com.au — Developmental Sequence of Phonological Skills](https://aussiechildcarenetwork.com.au/articles/child-development/developmental-sequence-of-phonological-skills) / [readnaturally.com — Phonological Awareness](https://www.readnaturally.com/research/5-components-of-reading/phonological-awareness) / [nspt4kids.com — Phonemic Awareness Skills by Age](https://www.nspt4kids.com/parenting/phonemic-awareness-skills-by-age)

### Two critical developmental qualifiers

**Recognition before manipulation:** Children can *recognize* phonological information before they can *produce or manipulate* it. A child can tell you which word doesn't rhyme before they can generate a rhyming word. A child can identify the first sound in "cat" before they can delete it and say "at."

**Blending before segmenting:** Within the same skill level, blending (combining given phonemes into a word) is consistently easier than segmenting (breaking a word into its phonemes). Segmenting requires simultaneous retrieval and decomposition; blending only requires synthesis. This is why Say-It-Move-It teaches blending and segmenting in the same activity — they are complementary but segmenting lags blending developmentally.

"Blending and segmentation are the phonemic awareness skills that have the most impact on later reading ability" — the broader phonological awareness hierarchy (rhyme, syllable, onset-rime) is prerequisite scaffolding, but blending and segmenting are the proximate causes of reading acquisition.
Source: [readnaturally.com](https://www.readnaturally.com/research/5-components-of-reading/phonological-awareness)

### Age-level skill floors with documentation

**Age 5 (typical kindergarten entry, September):**
- 61% can produce a rhyming word for a given stimulus (meaning 39% cannot yet rhyme productively at age 5)
- Beginning onset-rime awareness
- Only ~29% can blend individual phonemes into a word
- Only ~7% can fully segment a word into individual phonemes
- CVC words are at or slightly above the floor for most 5-year-olds

Source: [speechandlanguagekids.com — Ultimate Guide to Phonological Awareness](https://www.speechandlanguagekids.com/ultimate-guide-phonological-awareness-pre-reading-skills/) (citing Chaney, Snider)

**Age 5–6 (mid-to-late kindergarten):**
- Can blend and segment words with 3–4 phonemes including simple clusters (e.g., "hand" = /h/ /æ/ /n/ /d/)
- Can reliably identify initial and final sounds in words
- Can blend phonemes into a short word when given phonemes by the teacher
- Source: [nspt4kids.com — Phonemic Awareness Skills by Age](https://www.nspt4kids.com/parenting/phonemic-awareness-skills-by-age)

**Age 6–7 (Grade 1):**
- Full phoneme blending and segmentation expected as a benchmarked skill
- Beginning phoneme deletion (say "cat" without /k/ → "at") and substitution
- Can count phonemes in a word
- Beginning phonetic spelling: spells "cat" as "kat" or "ct" — both demonstrate phonological awareness in operation
- Source: [nspt4kids.com](https://www.nspt4kids.com/parenting/phonemic-awareness-skills-by-age)

### Instructional sequence implication

Games targeting age 5 should not assume phoneme segmentation is present. The game should be designed so that:
- A child who cannot yet segment can still succeed with scaffolded prompts (hearing the phonemes spoken one by one before placing)
- A child who can segment does so independently and receives the full orthographic mapping benefit

Children age 6–7 should be able to segment and select tiles independently for 3-letter CVC words. Phoneme manipulation tasks (needed for 4-letter words with consonant clusters) are appropriate from age 6–7 onward.
