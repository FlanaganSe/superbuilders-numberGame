Below is a BrainLift-ready research map for **Superbuilders**. I focused on the overlap that matters most for your product: **early numeracy, tangible/manipulative learning, guided play, educational app design, home math interaction, spatial/embodied cognition, and evaluation of early-childhood apps**. My strongest read of the field is that this kind of product can be genuinely valuable, but only if it behaves less like an **answer detector** and more like a **guided-play math environment** that makes **magnitude, part-whole structure, and strategy use** visible. Early math matters a lot, guided play outperforms more didactic approaches in some early-math outcomes, manipulatives help only under the right conditions, and the best math apps combine **feedback + progression/levelling**, not just fun. ([PubMed][1])

## The 12 sources I would read first

1. **Duncan et al. (2007), “School readiness and later achievement.”** Across six longitudinal studies, school-entry math was the strongest predictor of later achievement among the readiness skills they examined. This is the clearest high-level justification for caring deeply about whether Superbuilders changes actual math understanding, not just engagement. ([PubMed][1])

2. **National Research Council / National Academies, *Mathematics Learning in Early Childhood: Paths Toward Excellence and Equity* (2009).** This is one of the most authoritative field overviews on early-childhood mathematics learning, equity, and instruction. It is a strong anchor text for your Knowledge Tree. ([Math4All][2])

3. **NAEYC + NCTM, “Early Childhood Mathematics: Promoting Good Beginnings.”** This joint position statement describes what high-quality mathematics education for ages 3–6 should look like and lays out 10 research-based recommendations. It is useful for defining “developmentally appropriate” in your BrainLift. 

4. **IES / What Works Clearinghouse, *Teaching Math to Young Children*.** This practice guide gives five evidence-rated recommendations for preschool, pre-K, and kindergarten math instruction. It is one of the best “what should the product actually do?” documents in the whole stack. ([Institute of Education Sciences][3])

5. **Clements & Sarama’s Learning Trajectories / Building Blocks work.** Their line of work treats early math as a sequence of developmental progressions and intentionally sequenced activities. Building Blocks explicitly combines whole-group, small-group, center, computer, and family activities around learning trajectories. This is probably the single most relevant research program for your content design. ([Learning Trajectories][4])

6. **Carbonneau, Marley, & Selig (2013), meta-analysis on concrete manipulatives.** This meta-analysis found manipulatives outperform abstract-symbol-only instruction on average, but the effects are moderated by instructional design. The takeaway is not “physical is always better”; it is “physical can help when the representation is doing the right cognitive work.” ([ERIC][5])

7. **Cheung et al. (2023) / related concreteness work on perceptually rich manipulatives.** A key caution: perceptually rich materials can distract and may produce smaller retention effects or even worse problem solving than blander manipulatives. For Superbuilders, that means tile design and play-surface design matter a lot. ([ScienceDirect][6])

8. **Skene et al. (2022), meta-analysis on guided play.** Guided play showed a greater positive effect than direct instruction on early math skills, shape knowledge, and task switching, and outperformed free play on spatial vocabulary. This is a major clue about your product stance: preserve agency, but guide attention. 

9. **Ramani & Siegler on linear numerical board games.** Four 15-minute sessions of a simple linear number board game eliminated low- vs. middle-income differences in numerical estimation proficiency, and the work ties linear magnitude representations to broader math outcomes. This is extremely relevant to how you lay out tiles and problems on the surface. ([Dr. Robert Siegler][7])

10. **Schiffman et al. / linear-spatial materials for addition strategies.** Linear-spatial materials were more likely than irregular arrays to support better addition strategies like count-on, not just count-all. This is one of the most actionable findings for a tile-based system. ([PLOS][8])

11. **Outhwaite et al. (2019), randomized controlled trial of interactive math apps.** After a 12-week intervention, structured, content-rich interactive apps produced greater learning gains than standard math practice and generalized to higher-level reasoning/problem solving. This is strong evidence that well-designed apps can help, not just entertain. ([ERIC][9])

12. **Outhwaite et al. (2023), content analysis + qualitative comparative analysis of evaluated math apps.** This is one of the most practically useful app papers I found. Their analysis found that the combination of **explanatory + motivational feedback** with **programmatic levelling** (static or dynamic) was a necessary condition for highly effective math apps. They also note that most evaluated apps were practice-based, mostly targeted basic number skills, and only two included support for adult–child interactions. ([UCL Discovery][10])

A near-miss for the top 12, but very important: **Bang et al. on My Math Academy**. This personalized, mastery-based, adaptive system improved early math while keeping children engaged, and its gains were strongest where there was more room to grow and on harder skills. It is highly relevant for your adaptivity model. ([Springer Link][11])

Also crucial for the social layer: **parent number talk is causally relevant**, **parent process-vs-person responses matter**, and **math anxiety is visible already in kindergarten**. Those three facts should shape both adult prompts and error-handling in the product. 

## What the research implies for Superbuilders

**1. The physical tiles should not just confirm answers. They should externalize mathematical structure.**
The strongest design direction is to make placement itself meaningful: linear arrangements, visible composition/decomposition, comparing two quantities, and “make the same total in two ways” tasks. The literature on linear number games, linear-spatial materials, and part-whole relations all points in the same direction: children learn more when the representation reveals the math, not when it merely decorates it. ([Dr. Robert Siegler][7])

**2. Build around a learning trajectory, not a bag of arithmetic prompts.**
Clements/Sarama’s work and the WWC guide both push toward sequenced developmental progressions. A product for ages 5–8 should probably start from magnitude, counting principles, comparison, composition/decomposition, and then move into more fluent addition/subtraction strategies. My Math Academy’s mastery-based model is useful here: pre-assess, place learners appropriately, scaffold, then advance. ([Institute of Education Sciences][12])

**3. Your feedback model should be more than celebration.**
The Outhwaite app-analysis work is unusually direct: explanatory feedback plus motivational feedback plus levelling is what showed up in the effective apps. Explanatory feedback helps children make more deliberate decisions and reduce trial-and-error, especially early; later, detailed feedback can fade as mastery grows. That suggests a model like: early levels explain why, later levels celebrate and move on faster. ([UCL Discovery][10])

**4. Guided play is a better lens than either “free play” or “drill app.”**
The guided-play meta-analysis suggests that the strongest version of this product is one where the child has agency, but the environment strongly channels attention toward the mathematical idea. Superbuilders should feel playful, but it should also quietly shape what the child notices and tries next. 

**5. Capture strategies, not just correctness.**
Early addition does not move magically from wrong to right; children tend to move from count-all toward count-on, decomposition, and retrieval. Linear-spatial materials increase the use of more advanced strategies. A camera-based tangible system has an unusual opportunity here: it can potentially log order of tile placement, hesitation, self-correction, rearrangement, and alternate solutions. That is pedagogically richer than final-answer accuracy. ([PLOS][8])

**6. Add a home/teacher interaction layer, but do it carefully.**
Parent number talk can causally improve children’s number knowledge, yet newer work shows that simple informational priming can increase math talk while also increasing parental control and reducing autonomy support. Existing evaluated math apps rarely support adult–child interaction, which makes this a genuine product opportunity, but the prompts should be light-touch and process-oriented rather than controlling. 

**7. Mathematical language is part of the math, not just the UI.**
Math language—terms like many, most, fewest, before, after, near, far—predicts children’s math development. There is also evidence that children’s language proficiency matters in app-based math learning in bilingual settings. So the best version of Superbuilders probably teaches vocabulary and relationships, not just symbol manipulation. ([DOI][13])

**8. Hands matter in more ways than “manipulative.”**
Gesture research shows that asking children to gesture during math explanation can reveal implicit knowledge and support learning. Finger-training research suggests hands can bridge verbal, symbolic, and non-symbolic number representations. Your product already uses hands; the question is whether it uses them pedagogically or only mechanically. ([PubMed][14])

**9. Spatial design is probably underappreciated in your product.**
Early spatial training transfers to math, spatial assembly relates to emerging math as early as age 3, and semi-structured block play shows promising links to numeracy, math language, and executive function. Tile shape, spacing, and left-to-right layout may matter more than most teams assume. ([DOI][15])

**10. Engagement is not a proxy for educational value.**
A 2024 study found that preschoolers were less likely to prefer and repeatedly use apps that experts rated as having higher educational value. That is a huge unknown-unknown for product teams: the most “sticky” version of an app may not be the most educational version. Learning experiments need outcomes beyond retention/usage. ([ScienceDirect][16])

**11. Treat recognition errors as a learning-science problem, not just a CV problem.**
This is my inference from the literature, not a direct one-paper finding: if immediate feedback matters, and if math anxiety/self-concept already matter in kindergarten, then false negatives from the camera may be interpreted by children as “I’m bad at math,” not “the system misread my tile.” Child-facing UX should default to uncertainty-reduction language (“Hold still,” “Let’s check again”) rather than hard-error language when confidence is low. ([UCL Discovery][10])

**12. The tangible-interface evidence is promising, but still thinner than the broader early-math literature.**
Tangible-user-interface research argues that TUIs may support early math because they combine physical objects with multimedia and can support home math interaction, and reviews suggest the space is growing. But the evidence base is still emerging, which means you should validate Superbuilders empirically rather than assuming “physical + digital” is automatically superior. ([ACM Digital Library][17])

## Unknown unknowns that deserve real investigation

1. **Is the physicality doing cognitive work, or is it just novelty?**
   Manipulatives help on average, but not automatically; richness can distract, and aligned spatial structure matters. ([ERIC][5])

2. **Are you teaching part-whole reasoning, or only answer production?**
   A 2025 review found that none of the 18 reviewed math apps implemented a systematic approach to part-whole learning from hands-on to more abstract compositions/decompositions. That looks like a real market gap. ([DOI Resolver][29])

3. **Can your product actually move children off count-all strategies?**
   Count-all is an early strategy, but more advanced strategies like count-on, decomposition, and retrieval matter for later success. Tile sequencing and surface design should be tested against that goal. ([PLOS][8])

4. **Which levelling regime helps which child?**
   Dynamic and static programmatic levelling both appear in effective apps, but lower-ability children may benefit more from structured static pathways while higher-ability children may benefit more from dynamic skipping/acceleration. ([UCL Discovery][10])

5. **How much explanation is enough before it becomes drag?**
   Explanatory feedback helps novices, but detailed feedback becomes less necessary once the task is mastered. The fade schedule itself is a product hypothesis you should test. ([UCL Discovery][10])

6. **Will parent prompts help, or quietly harm autonomy?**
   Parent number talk can help, but informational priming can increase controlling behavior and reduce autonomy support. Adult scaffolds need careful wording. 

7. **Are you building mathematical language as well as calculation?**
   Math language predicts math development, and app-based math learning varies with language proficiency in bilingual settings. That makes wording, narration, and bilingual support more important than many teams assume. ([DOI][13])

8. **Will children naturally choose the educationally strongest version of the game?**
   Probably not. Children can prefer apps with lower expert-rated educational value. You need A/B tests that include learning outcomes, not just time-on-task. ([ScienceDirect][16])

9. **How will you separate “camera uncertainty” from “math error” in the child’s mind?**
   Again, this is an inference, but a very important one. Early math affect matters, and child-facing feedback is powerful. Recognition confidence should shape UX. ([DOI][30])

10. **What is your transfer story?**
    You should measure near transfer, far transfer, and delayed retention. The best app studies report generalized gains, but many apps in the field are still basic-skill/practice-heavy. ([ERIC][9])

11. **Can the product support inclusion and trust?**
    There is tangible-game work on math + sign language in inclusive settings, and app-evaluation research explicitly includes security and parental control as dimensions. Your no-cloud architecture could be a trust advantage if you treat it as part of the pedagogy, not just the stack. ([DOI][31])

12. **Does the game increase what children spontaneously notice about number?**
    SFON—spontaneous focusing on numerosity—relates to early numerical skills and later arithmetic indirectly through magnitude estimation and calculation. A really strong product might change what children notice in everyday scenes, not just how they answer prompted items. ([DOI][32])

## BrainLift-ready first draft

### Purpose

The purpose of this BrainLift is to determine how a **camera-based tangible arithmetic game for ages 5–8** can be designed so that physical interaction, feedback, and progression **measurably improve early numeracy** rather than merely increasing engagement.

### In Scope

* Early numeracy and school-readiness research
* Learning trajectories for counting, magnitude, composition/decomposition, and early addition/subtraction
* Manipulatives, spatial/embodied learning, gesture, and hands-on representation
* Guided play, home math environment, parent/teacher prompts, and math language
* Educational app design: feedback, levelling, adaptivity, assessment, and transfer
* Inclusion, parental control, trust, and privacy in early-childhood technology

### Out of Scope

* Generic “edtech is good/bad” debates without age-specific math evidence
* Product metrics that ignore actual learning outcomes
* Pure CV/ML optimization divorced from the child’s learning experience
* Treating delight, session length, or installation rate as proof of learning

### DOK 4 — Candidate Spiky POVs

**SPOV 1: The moat is not computer vision; the moat is representational fidelity.**
A better detector does not automatically produce better learning. The research suggests that the benefit of physical materials depends on whether they make mathematical structure visible. Linear/spatial alignment, part-whole relations, and decomposition opportunities matter more than “real object” status alone. In practice, that means Superbuilders wins when tile placement reveals number relationships, not when the CV stack merely reads answers faster. ([ERIC][5])

**SPOV 2: The right product stance is guided play, not drill and not free play.**
The strongest design pattern in this literature is child agency inside an intentionally scaffolded path. Guided play outperforms more didactic formats on some early-math outcomes, and the strongest app results come from systems that blend playfulness with explanatory feedback and progression. Superbuilders should feel open-ended enough to invite exploration, but structured enough to teach. 

**SPOV 3: The highest-leverage data are not right/wrong answers; they are strategy traces.**
Most products stop at correctness. A camera-based tangible system can go further by observing strategy changes, hesitation, self-correction, alternative decompositions, and adult-child interaction patterns. That is a more defensible educational instrument and a stronger long-term product differentiator than “we do on-device inference in Safari.” ([PLOS][8])

### DOK 3 — Candidate Insights

* Early math is one of the highest-leverage places to intervene because school-entry math is a strong predictor of later achievement. ([PubMed][1])
* Tangible materials help when they map clearly onto the target idea; perceptually rich or poorly aligned materials can hurt learning or transfer. ([ERIC][5])
* A tile-based math game should teach **part-whole reasoning** explicitly, because this is crucial in early arithmetic and under-supported in current apps. ([DOI Resolver][29])
* Explanatory feedback matters most early, while purely motivational feedback is not enough for novices. ([UCL Discovery][10])
* Adaptive placement and developmental sequencing are likely more important than adding more content volume. ([UCL Discovery][10])
* Parent/teacher prompts should be process-oriented and autonomy-supportive; more math talk is not always better if it becomes controlling. 
* Mathematical language and bilingual language support are not secondary UX issues; they are part of the learning mechanism. ([DOI][13])
* Engagement cannot be treated as equivalent to educational quality. ([ScienceDirect][16])

### DOK 2 — Knowledge Tree seed

* Duncan et al. (2007) — early math at school entry was the strongest predictor of later achievement among the readiness skills studied. Summary: early numeracy is a high-leverage target. ([PubMed][1])

* Clements & Sarama / Building Blocks / Learning Trajectories — intentionally sequenced developmental progressions. Summary: content order matters. ([Institute of Education Sciences][12])

**Category 3: How physical interaction supports or harms learning**

* Carbonneau et al. (2013) — manipulatives help on average but effects depend on design. Summary: physicality is conditional. ([ERIC][5])
* Cheung et al. (2023) / related richness studies — perceptually rich manipulatives can distract. Summary: tile aesthetics and surface design are not cosmetic. ([ScienceDirect][6])
* Linear number games / linear-spatial materials — linear layouts support magnitude representations and better strategies. Summary: surface geometry is instructional. ([Dr. Robert Siegler][7])

**Category 4: Play, home interaction, and social mediation**

* Skene et al. (2022) — guided play has stronger effects than direct instruction on some early-math outcomes. Summary: playful structure beats pure drill. 
* Eason et al. / parent number talk intervention — parent number talk can causally affect number knowledge. Summary: home prompts matter.

**Category 5: What makes math apps effective**

* Outhwaite et al. (2019) — structured interactive math apps improved outcomes. Summary: good apps can teach. ([ERIC][9])
* Outhwaite et al. (2023) — explanatory feedback + motivational feedback + levelling was a necessary condition in highly effective apps. Summary: feedback architecture and progression are core product decisions. ([UCL Discovery][10])
* Bang et al. / My Math Academy — mastery-based, adaptive, embedded-assessment design improved learning and engagement. Summary: adaptivity + assessment + challenge calibration is a strong template. ([Springer Link][11])

**Category 6: Language, inclusion, and trust**

* Mathematical language research — math language predicts development. Summary: narration and vocabulary are instructional features. ([DOI][13])
* Bilingual app learning / DLL work — language proficiency matters in app-based math instruction. Summary: build for multilingual reality. ([Bera Journals][33])
* NAEYC tech guidance + app-evaluation research — technology is effective when used intentionally; security and parental control matter. Summary: your no-cloud stance is an asset only if paired with thoughtful design. ([University of Oklahoma][34])
